# ADR-006 — The catalogue prices the order, not the customer

- **Status:** Accepted
- **Date:** 7.14.2026
- **Deciders:** Robert Felkins
- **Related:** ADR-001 (saga), ADR-004 (service boundaries)

## Context

The original `PlaceOrderViewModel` carried a `UnitPrice` on every line. The client sent it, `ToDomain` copied it onto the aggregate, `Subtotal` and `Total` were computed from it, and the saga put that total into `ChargePayment.Amount`.

Which means **the customer set the price they were charged.** A caller could order a laptop for a penny by editing one field of the JSON they were already sending, and nothing downstream would have noticed — every service faithfully carried the number it was given, because every service was written to trust its input. There was no validation that could have caught it either, because there was nothing to validate *against*: no service in the system knew what anything cost.

That is the root of it. The price was missing from the domain entirely, not merely mis-sourced.

## Decision

**Inventory owns the catalogue, so Inventory owns the price.**

- `StockItem` gains `UnitPrice`. This is the only price in the system.
- `OrderLineViewModel` **loses** `UnitPrice`. The client sends a SKU and a quantity. That is all it is entitled to assert.
- A placed order is **unpriced**: `Subtotal`, `Total` and every line's `UnitPrice` are zero, and `OrderPlaced` carries no total at all.
- `InventoryReserved` carries the priced lines and the order `Total`, read off the `StockItem` rows at the moment the hold was taken.
- The saga stamps those prices onto the aggregate and charges `order.Total` — a number that came from the catalogue, never from the customer.

## Consequences

**Positive**

- The attack is gone, not mitigated. A field that does not exist cannot be forged, and there is no validation rule to get subtly wrong.
- The price is read at the moment stock is held, from the row that was held, in the service that owns it. There is no window where the price could drift between quoting and charging.
- It falls out of the existing message flow. No new service call, no HTTP hop between Orders and Inventory, no shared database. The reply the saga was already waiting for just carries more information.

**Negative (accepted)**

- **An order exists, briefly, with a total of zero.** Between `OrderPlaced` and `InventoryReserved` the status view will show a £0 order. This looks like a bug and is not: the customer has said what they want, and nobody has yet said what it costs. The alternative — showing a number the customer supplied — is a lie that happens to look tidier.
- **The customer is not shown a price before they commit.** A real checkout quotes first. That needs a `GET /api/Inventory` price lookup from the UI before placing the order, and a decision about what happens when the price moves between the quote and the reservation. Out of scope here; the failure paths are the subject.
- **Inventory rejects an unknown SKU rather than an unpriced one.** The two are the same condition, which is fine now and would not be in a system where a SKU can exist without a price.

## Alternatives considered

- **Keep `UnitPrice` on the ViewModel and validate it server-side.** Rejected. It requires the price to exist somewhere anyway — so you do all the work of this ADR and then *also* keep the forged field around, plus a comparison that has to decide what to do when they disagree. Strictly worse.
- **A separate pricing/catalogue service.** The correct answer at scale, and over-engineering here. It would add a sixth service whose only job is a lookup, and the SKU price is already a property of the thing Inventory is holding.
- **Orders calls Inventory over HTTP to price the order before placing it.** Rejected: it puts a synchronous dependency on the front of an architecture whose entire thesis is that services meet on the bus and never in the request path. A slow Inventory would then make placing an order slow, which is precisely what the saga exists to avoid.
- **Record it as a POC simplification and move on.** Rejected. This is a reference architecture people are meant to read and copy. Shipping a documented "the client sets the price" is shipping a bug with a footnote.
