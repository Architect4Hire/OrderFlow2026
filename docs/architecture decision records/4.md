# ADR-004 — No separate warehouse-management service

- **Status:** Accepted
- **Date:** 7.13.2026
- **Deciders:** Robert Felkins
- **Related:** ADR-001 (saga), design principle "right-sizing is an architectural decision"

## Context

A real fulfillment platform eventually grows a warehouse-management subsystem — pick/pack workflows, bin locations, carrier rate shopping, label generation. It is tempting to model that as its own service to make the architecture look "complete." The question for this reference architecture is whether decomposing fulfillment further teaches anything the diagram doesn't already show, or whether it is resume-driven padding that dilutes the one idea the system exists to demonstrate.

This ADR exists specifically to record a decision **not** to build something — the clearest expression of the "right-sizing is an architectural decision" thesis.

## Decision

**Do not** create a separate warehouse-management service. Fulfillment is a single service whose job is to call a (simulated) warehouse/carrier with resilience — retry with backoff, circuit breaking, and dead-lettering on hard failure — and to report `FulfillmentDispatched` or `FulfillmentFailed` back to the saga. The warehouse/carrier itself is represented as a **simulated external system**, not an internal container.

## Consequences

**Positive**

- The demo stays focused on what it proves — the saga, compensation, idempotency, and resilience — instead of spreading attention across a subsystem that adds surface without adding insight.
- One fewer service to build, deploy, observe, and keep consistent. The Fulfillment service's resilience story (Polly + dead-letter) is fully demonstrable without it.
- The seam is honest: dispatch is an outbound call to something we don't own, which is exactly how it behaves in production.

**Negative (accepted)**

- The system is **not feature-complete** as a fulfillment platform. A viewer expecting pick/pack/ship internals won't find them. We accept this — completeness is not the goal; demonstrating correct distributed-systems behavior is.
- If a real engagement genuinely needed warehouse management, this boundary would have to be revisited. The decision is right for the POC, not a universal law — and it is recorded here precisely so a future reader knows it was a choice, not an oversight.

## Alternatives considered

- **Decompose fulfillment into a warehouse-management service** (and possibly further into picking, packing, shipping). Rejected. It would add services whose internal behavior is out of scope, and every one would need its own domain, data, and consumers — cost with no demonstrative payoff. This is the exact over-decomposition the "right-sizing" principle warns against.
- **Fold fulfillment entirely into the Order service.** Rejected in the other direction — Fulfillment earns its own service because its resilient-outbound-call behavior (retry, circuit break, dead-letter) is a distinct concern worth isolating and demonstrating.

## Verification

**Verified:** `Fulfillment.API/Managers/Business/CarrierClient.cs` — the carrier is a simulated external system behind `ICarrierClient`, wrapped in a Polly pipeline (bounded retry with exponential backoff, plus a circuit breaker) tuned for this dependency rather than lifted from a global default. Retry and breaker share one predicate, `TransientCarrierException`, so a `PermanentCarrierException` falls straight through untouched — a rejected address does not get better because you asked four more times. `FulfillmentConsumers.cs` reports `FulfillmentDispatched` or `FulfillmentFailed` back to the saga and publishes nothing else. There is no warehouse service, no pick/pack domain, and no internal container pretending to be one. The boundary is exactly as described.

**Open mismatch — this ADR says "dead-lettering on hard failure," and the code deliberately does not do that.** The phrase conflates two failures that must end in opposite places, and taking it literally would produce the worst outcome the system can produce.

A **hard carrier failure** — retries exhausted, or a permanent rejection — has been *processed*. We asked, we got a final answer, and the answer was no. The consumer publishes `FulfillmentFailed` and **completes** the message; the saga then refunds the payment and releases the stock, and the order dies cleanly. Dead-lettering it instead would mean the saga is never told anything at all: money captured, stock held, order frozen at `Paid` forever, with nothing on any screen to say why.

Dead-lettering is reserved for failures that were **not** processed — a poison message, a reply that could not be published, a circuit still open past the delivery count. Those are the genuinely stuck orders, and they are what `GET /api/Fulfillment/stuck` and `GET /api/Orders/dead-letters` list.

So the code is right and the ADR's wording is wrong. Corrected, the sentence should read: *"…and dead-lettering on failures it cannot get an answer to."* A business outcome is not a delivery failure. Left as an open item rather than silently reworded, because the distinction is the whole point of the service.
